Reptiles

Reptilia are a paraphyletic class of tetrapods. The classical classification system distinguishes four living orders:

  • Turtles (Testudines) — approximately 300 species
  • Sphenodontia or Rhynchocephalia — tuatara: 2 species
  • Squamata — lizards, snakes and amphisbaenia: approximately 7,600 species
  • Crocodiles (Crocodilia) — 24 species
Turtles

Turtles (Testudines or Chelonia), formerly turtles or tortoises, are a monophyletic order of the class Reptilia. They are characterized by a box-like shell made of fused bones of the thoracic and pelvic girdle, which distinguishes them from all other vertebrates.

Helemászíkok

Helemászíkok (Rhynchocephalia or Sphenodontia) is an order belonging to the class of reptiles (Reptilia). Their exterior resembles that of lizards, but their internal structure bears the marks of various other orders and even classes. The order includes the hydras, often referred to as "living fossils".

Squamous Reptiles

Squamous reptiles (Squamata) are an order belonging to the class of reptiles. Their common characteristic is that their bodies are covered with scales, and their heads and ventral sides are covered with shields. The upper palate is such that the nostrils open at the very beginning of the oral cavity. There are approximately 7,600 species in the order.

Crocodiles

Crocodiles (Crocodylia) are an order of reptiles. Their closest living relatives are birds. Crocodiles belong to the subclass Archosauromorpha of the reptile class, and their closest relatives are the orders Aetosauria, Phytosauria, and Rauissuchia.
